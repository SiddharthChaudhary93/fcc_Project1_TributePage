Rabindranath Tagore

"Where the mind is without fear"

About

  • 1861 - Born in Calcutta, Bengal Presidency, British India.
  • 1873 - Leaves Calcutta for Dalhousie where he read biographies, studied history, astronomy, modern science, and Sanskrit, and examined the classical poetry of Kālidāsa.
  • 1877 - Returns to Jorosanko and completed a set of major works named Maithili in the style of Vidyapati.
  • 1882 - Debuted in the short-story genre in Bengali with "Bhikharini"("The Begger Woman").
  • 1878 - Enrolls at a public school in Brighton, East Sussex, England.
  • 1879 - Briefly read law at University College London, but again left school, opting instead for independent study of Shakespeare's plays Coriolanus, and Antony and Cleopatra and the Religio Medici of Thomas Browne.
  • 1880 - Returns to bengal degree-less.
  • 1883 - Gets married to Merinalini Devi.
  • 1890 - Begins managing his vast ancestoral estates in Shelaidaha and releases his Manasi poems.
  • 1891-1895 - He wrote more than half the stories of the three-volume, 84-story Galpaguchchha.
  • 1901 - Moved to Shantiniketan to found an ashram with a marble-floored prayer hall —The Mandir— an experimental school, groves of trees, gardens, a library.
  • 1901-1906 - He published Naivedya and Kheya and translated poems into free verse.
  • 1913 - Wins the Nobel Prize in Literature.
  • 1915 - Awarded a knighthood by King George V.
  • 1919 - Renounces the knighthood after the Jallianwala Bagh massacre.
  • 1921 - He and agricultural economist Leonard Elmhirst set up the "Institute for Rural Reconstruction", later renamed Shriniketan or "Abode of Welfare", in Surul, a village near the ashram.
  • 1932-1941 - Fifteen new volumes appeared, among them prose-poem works Punashcha (1932), Shes Saptak (1935), and Patraput (1936). Experimentation continued in his prose-songs and dance-dramas— Chitra (1914), Shyama (1939), and Chandalika (1938)— and in his novels— Dui Bon (1933), Malancha (1934), and Char Adhyay (1934).
  • 1941 - Dies at the age of 80.
  • 1941 - The 'Azad Hind' team formally decided that Tagore's 'Jana Gana Mana' will be the Indian national anthem.

I have given completely whatever I had to give. In return if I receive anything—some love, some forgiveness—then I will take it with me when I step on the boat that crosses to the festival of the wordless end.
